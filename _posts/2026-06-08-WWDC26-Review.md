Yet another year of WWDC, and things were run differently this year. Right away, one of the first things I noticed was that Apple didn’t introduce new software by device type, but grouped it into three main categories.
-	Platform improvements
-	Trust and safety
-	Apple Intelligence and Siri

It was interesting watching them group features together, jumping from MacOS to iOS then back to MacOS, but throughout the keynote, it made more sense to me as these systems have become more unified. 

## Platform Improvements:

I was pleased to hear that the iPhone 11 (from 2019) would still be receiving updates and that iOS 27 would improve system performance, as had been rumored. I also enjoyed all the refinements to liquid glass and immediately downloaded iPad OS 27 to try them out. Surprisingly, my old iPad mini didn’t feel like it slowed down at all, even though this was the first developer beta. It actually felt faster.

It was interesting how little Apple covered individual features this year, and it makes me excited for people to uncover new features and find them like easter eggs within the coming days (although I do know it is because this year was focused on performance and AI).

I found it really interesting that, with Apple supporting all iPhones in the next version of iOS, but it will only support the Apple Watch Series 9 and up. This means the first-generation Apple Watch Ultra will be discontinued after just 4 years. I can’t imagine how Siri would be the reason for this, since you would still need an Apple Intelligence-powered device to run these complex Siri features on your watch, but I may be wrong. If this isn’t the case, though, Apple could have done what it does with its iPhones, omitting Apple Intelligence or Siri AI on older Apple Watches. It is interesting because not many features have been introduced for the watch this year, yet so many devices were cut from support.

## Trust and Safety:

Moving on to trust and safety, I’ve never seen Apple dive so deeply into this category. It was nice to hear, but it didn’t specifically interest me since I have no children and am a college student. I do like that Apple is taking such a stand on these issues and working with many different organizations to show they aren’t just implementing features but understanding and researching how to best help families.

## Apple Intelligence and Siri:

Finally, the part of the keynote that everyone, from investors to your average consumer who bought an iPhone 16 in 2024, is for Apple's AI (me). Apple unveiled some very promising features with demos that looked real, and I was impressed. Siri can now actually pull from personal context; it has its own dedicated app and a new design that is less in-your-face. I like how Apple is so dedicated to its local AI models and privacy. It is something I really admire, and even though I think it is partly because they are falling behind, it is an approach that I feel makes the average consumer more accepting of AI on their phone. 

It does suck that certain features like a personalized Siri voice are only available to iPhone 17 Pro and iPhone Air. I really do think that Apple should have given that to all Apple Intelligence supported devices, especially since so many are upset with the flop Apple Intelligence has been over the past two years.

I'm also a little confused about Apple introducing Siri AI on top of Apple Intelligence and the original Siri. I wonder if the AI in Siri stands for Artificial Intelligence or Apple Intelligence. 
Finally, I noticed how, for macOS, Siri has taken over Spotlight search. This is something I think I was a little skeptical of, too, since I am scared it will slow Spotlight down, and I am confused by the fact that the App Library was connected to Spotlight in Mac OS Tahoe, so how will that operate too now? Hopefully, they made it faster and stopped the icons from taking forever to load. I also wonder if this will replace Spotlight search on iOS and iPad OS too?

All of these wonders will be figured out in due time when I get to try and review all of these new operating systems on my main devices, since I sadly don’t have extra supported Apple devices lying around, only vintage ones.

## Overall Takeaways:

This year’s WWDC was different, and I think Apple was trying to make up for many of its past mistakes. But again, I feel like people came in expecting more than they got, even though we all knew this would be a “Snow Leopard” update. Tim Cook will be missed but, but I wonder how Apple will structure it’s future events with John Turnes stepping in as new CEO.
